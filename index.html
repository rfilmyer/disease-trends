US Diseases and Vaccinations

A hundred years ago, diseases like smallpox, polio, and measles ran rampant across America. The development and deployment of large-scale vaccination programs across the country helped banish these diseases from the United States, and in the case of smallpox, extinguish it entirely. Two of America's success stories are with the smallpox and the MMR (Mumps, Measles, and Rubella) vaccine.

Smallpox

Image from Brooklyn Historical Society.

Christopher Columbus's 1492 expedition first brought smallpox to the New World, and it was not until 1952 that the disease was finally eradicated from the Americas. While smallpox was one of the first diseases to have a vaccine developed, with experiments occuring as early as 1796, it took over a century to substantially reduce cases in America. By the 20th century, mass vaccination campaigns had largely controlled the disease in the US, with the last endemic case occurring in 1952. [Source]

Mumps, Measles, and Rubella

"It was terrible,"

— Beth Lyon, victim of a 1977 Des Moines measles outbreak. [Source]

Before a measles vaccine became available in 1963, an estimated 3 to 4 million Americans a year contracted the disease. Nearly every child contracted the disease by the time they turned 15 years old.[Source] Around the same time, rubella, or German measles, was first isolated. When an outbreak of the disease, which disproportionately affects pregnant mothers and infants, struck North America and Western Europe in 1963–5, serious efforts began towards vaccination, and a vaccine was available by 1970. [Source] Mumps, another common childhood disease, had a vaccine developed by 1968.[Source] These three vaccines were packaged as a single "MMR" vaccine, and with the development of a chicken pox (varicella) vaccine in 1995, as an "MMRV" vaccine.

Mumps, Measles, Rubella, and the MMR Vaccine

By the early 1970s, vaccines for mumps, measles, and rubella were commercially available. Since then, the diseases have been eliminated or nearly eliminated in the US.
